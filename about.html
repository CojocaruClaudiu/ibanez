About Ibanez

Ibanez (アイバニーズ, Aibanīzu) is a renowned Japanese guitar brand owned by Hoshino Gakki. Based in Nagoya, Aichi, Japan, Hoshino Gakki became pioneers in the Japanese music industry. They gained significant recognition in import guitar sales across the United States and Europe. Ibanez holds the distinction of being the first brand to mass-produce seven-string and eight-string guitars.


Ibanez's global footprint spans Japan, China, Indonesia, and the United States, with a custom shop in Los Angeles. As of 2017, their extensive lineup included nearly 165 models of bass guitars, 130 acoustic guitars, and over 300 electric guitars. Today, Ibanez stands proudly as the third-largest guitar brand, following in the footsteps of industry giants Gibson and Fender.


The story begins in 1908 when Hoshino Gakki started as the musical instrument sales division of the Hoshino Shoten, a Japanese bookstore chain. The journey involved importing Spanish-style acoustic guitars, initially branded "Ibanez Salvador" to honor Spanish luthier Salvador Ibáñez. Over time, the brand evolved, changing its spelling to "Ibanez" in 1986, reflecting an English pronunciation.

Hoshino Gakki faced a legal challenge from Gibson Guitars in 1977, accusing them of using the Gibson headstock design and logo. The case was settled out of court in early 1978. Post-lawsuit, Ibanez shifted its strategy, moving away from copying classic electric guitar designs. They introduced a range of original designs, including the Artist models, Performer, Concert, Musicians, Iceman, Roadster, and Roadstar, competing with and surpassing famous American brands.


Embracing modern elements, Ibanez incorporated radical body shapes, slimmer necks, 2-octave fingerboards, pointed headstocks, high-output electronics, and various pickup configurations. This marked the beginning of a new era, influencing the popular superstrat trend in the mid-1980s.
